Fundamental 1

Know Where You're Going

Marketing without a destination is just activity. This is your vision, your goal, and the one thing you want the market to know you for.

Marketing can't be aligned to business intent that was never written down — and in most audits we run, it never was. AI changes the research: the outside-in view that took a firm weeks now takes hours. What it cannot do is set your goals, your risk appetite, or your definition of "worth it." The destination is a leadership act.

One warning before you use AI here: it will happily validate whatever direction you feed it — it tells you what you want to hear. A leader decides when the plan is real and when it's just agreement dressed up as analysis.

Fundamental 2

Understand Your Buyer

Everything downstream breaks if you're guessing here. Get specific about who buys — and the moment they decide to.

Most companies build their buyer picture in a conference room. Our CMOs build it from real buyer interviews, organized into the 5 Buying Insights: the Priority Initiative (the trigger event), Success Factors, Perceived Barriers, Decision Criteria, and the Buyer Journey — all in the buyer's verbatim words.

Two patterns surprise almost every founder: switching pain and onboarding effort kill more deals than price, and referred buyers quietly "scrub everything" online before they ever call you. The best interview opener we know:

"Walk me back to the moment you started looking. What happened?"

Fundamental 3

Position Your Offer

The most visible fundamental — it's what the market actually sees. This is where most businesses are strong and buried at the same time.

Being good at what you do is a baseline, not a differentiator. Buyers decide on outcomes — responsiveness, clarity, proactive planning, ease of switching — so the test is whether your marketing is outcome-forward or brand-forward: are you answering the buyer's decision criteria, or talking about yourself?

And there's a new checkpoint on the validation pathway: buyers now ask AI who to hire. Only about 7% notice a vendor in an AI answer because of the brand name — the firm that fits beats the firm that's famous. If answer engines can't see you, you fail the validation step without ever knowing it happened.

Fundamental 4

Sales & Marketing Alignment

Marketing hands the baton to sales. If that handoff is fumbled, spend leaks out.

In our audits, this fundamental fails one specific way most often: nothing is documented. No sales flow, no shared buyer definition, no joint review. Alignment isn't a feeling between departments — it's a set of artifacts both sides can point to.

Why it's urgent now: 95% of deals are won by a vendor already on the buyer's Day-One shortlist — formed before any sales contact. The deal is shaped before sales ever sees it. Marketing is your early sales team, whether you run it that way or not.

Fundamental 5

Cadence for Planning & Accountability

Strategy dies without rhythm. This is the operating system that keeps marketing accountable.

Growth is a discipline, not a project — and the discipline runs on two rhythms: quarterly (set the goal, define the Rocks, document the campaign plans) and weekly (scorecard, rock status, issues). Cadence dies in most companies because it's tedious. AI removes the tedium — it pulls the numbers, fills the scorecard, flags anomalies, drafts the update before the meeting.

What AI cannot do is own the number. Every rock and every metric carries a person's name — a name, not a department. And don't skip the review nobody schedules: vendor accountability. Undocumented vendor expectations are how firms get burned.

The 1–3 priorities that matter most.
The 3–5 numbers you'll watch weekly to know if it's working.
When do you plan, review, and hold team/vendors accountable?
The market reads silence as a signal.

What a CMO would look for:
  • Well-formed rocks: specific, done/not-done by quarter end, one named owner each
  • A weekly scorecard of lead and lag indicators, reviewable in 20 minutes
  • Vendor accountability: documented expectations and a results review — undocumented vendor expectations are how firms get burned
Artifact this produces: quarterly rock(s) with owners + weekly scorecard + vendor review cadence.

Fundamental 6

Structured Approach to Advertising

Advertising should be a system you evaluate, not a bill you pay on faith.

This is where AI changes the most — content, campaigns, and creative are food for AI, and the cost of producing them is collapsing toward zero. Which is exactly why the discipline matters more, not less: automating bad marketing doesn't fix it — it fails faster and louder.

Every campaign runs through a creative brief that forces five decisions before anything gets made: goal, audience, offer, creative & channels, and success metrics — measured on business outcomes (CPA, leads, pipeline, profitability), not clicks. The rule for everything AI produces for you: stickier, not ickier. Ickier is spam at scale and outreach that reads automated. Stickier is more relevant, more personal, more worth keeping — the machine does the grunt work, a human does the judgment.

Native lens — CPA & profitabilityEvery tactic earns its place against cost-per-acquisition and profit — not vibes.

  • Business-outcome measurement — CPA, leads, pipeline, profitability. Clicks and engagement are activity, not measurement
  • The rule: AI should make your marketing stickier, not ickier
